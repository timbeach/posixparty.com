where we are

The full pipeline (text → AI voice → AI character → talking-head video → spliced reaction clip) is working end-to-end on open-source models running on a single 24GB cloud GPU. This is a pipeline proof-of-concept, not shippable content. What's in §5 and §6 below is the cheapest possible end-to-end demonstration — a floating talking-head bust against whatever background the bible image happened to have. To get to shippable we still need the character composited into a believable creator-setup environment (at a desk, in a real-looking room, ideally with the source clip on a screen they're reacting to), tighter animation framing, expression-range fixes (§7 D1), and better motion/body language. Voice is the only piece that's actually at shipping quality today.

4. what we rejected — SDXL + InstantID (Tier 1)

Yesterday's first attempt before switching to FLUX. Same 12-image matrix, identity-preserving via InstantID. Disqualified for two reasons.

Disqualified for:
  1. Getty Images watermarks bleeding through on multiple images (SDXL training-data leak — see laughing/surprised shots).
  2. Waxy/plastic skin on expressive shots — uncanny-valley territory; not "real human" enough.
  3. Identity drift: keyframe had long blonde hair with bangs, bible drifted to pixie cut on most images.
Switching to FLUX-dev cost ~$3 of compute (model downloads + iterations) but eliminated all three issues.

5. end-to-end pipeline validation

Bible image (still) + Mason Turbo audio → EchoMimic V3 → talking-head MP4 with lip-sync. This is the core loop the whole project rests on. Both characters tested with the same audio (the "amused" line) for direct comparison. What you're about to see is a bust against the bible image's original background — not a creator at a desk in a room. Treat it as proof the pipe works, not as a sample of final output.

Mason character (FLUX bible · laughing portrait) + Turbo audio
Synthetic character (FLUX v2 bible · laughing portrait) + same Turbo audio
What to listen + watch for (POC signal): mouth shapes match phonemes ("g", "K", "you have G[OT] to..."), eye contact, subtle head movement driven by audio energy. Identity stays locked from the bible image — no drift across the 3.24 seconds.

What's clearly NOT shippable yet: floating-bust framing with no body / no environment / no desk / no monitor / no creator-setup signals. Animation is just face + slight neck. Composited against the bible image's outdoor/indoor background, not a believable streamer-room scene. Motion library is limited. This is "the pipe works" not "the content is good."

6. PiP demos (yesterday's batch using Mason headshot directly)

8 reaction clips spliced into source videos in the SniperWolf PiP format. These were made BEFORE we had the FLUX bibles (the talking head is just mason_goth.jpeg animated by EchoMimic on each audio). These are scratch demos to feel the format and confirm the splice plumbing works — not anywhere near production quality. The talking head is a static portrait against a plain background; a real product needs the character in a creator setup (room, desk, lighting matching the source clip's energy, etc.).

Spec: talking head appears at the reaction timestamp from the JSON ("timestamp": "0:06"), bottom-right corner, 28% scale. Source audio dropped to 55% volume; reaction audio mixed in at full volume. ffmpeg overlay+amix in one pass.

7. open decisions for the team

Things that need a call before we scale up to producing real content.

D1 — Synthetic character: ship as-is or iterate further?Q1
v2 has narrow expression range outside of "laughing". Options: (a) ship and live with 80% of shots being slight-smile; (b) one more iteration with id_weight 0.4 + aggressive negatives; (c) build keyframe from a real stoic photo as PuLID reference. Cost per iteration: ~$1.50 + 90 min.
D2 — Voice strategy: only Mason, or build a small voice pool?Q2
Today everything uses Mason's clone. For multi-character content we'd want 2-4 voices. Each clone needs a 20s reference clip. Cost per new voice: trivial (no GPU, just record + commit ref).
D3 — Aesthetic direction beyond "candid amateur photo"?Q3
Current FLUX bibles look like real iPhone photos in real environments. Alternatives we deliberately ruled out today: editorial/studio (too polished, was the SDXL failure mode), VTuber/anime (Tim said no), polished influencer (would need different prompts + LoRA). Want to keep "candid amateur" or pivot?
D4 — Quality ceiling: stay on 4090 or graduate to H100 for Wan2.2?Q4
EchoMimic V3 Flash on 4090 is "good enough talking head". The May 2026 visual research flagged Wan2.2-S2V-14B and Wan2.2-Animate-14B as a quality tier above (full-body animation, relighting, character replacement on existing video). Both need H100 (~$2/hr). One-day spike to evaluate vs the current EchoMimic baseline = ~$16 + a half day of work. Not urgent but worth ranking.
D5 — How many bibles + reactions before launch?Q5
Pilot scale-out: pick 5 source clips, generate full reactions for each (line + audio + animated talking head + PiP composite) using the Mason bible. At current per-image cost ~7min FLUX + ~30s EchoMimic + audio (free), batch of 5 reactions ≈ 1 hour pod time + manual selection of bible shots. Could do 50/day if we automate pose-selection.
D6 — Public-facing brand vs. behind-the-scenes content splitQ6
Memory says "keep LHFAM secret, BTS content on IG/TikTok/Shorts to build AI expert presence". Confirm split: production reaction channel uses synthetic character as public face; we publish BTS process content separately under our real names?
